Exploring Causal Inference in Earth Science

Causal Inference Time Series Analysis R Earth Science Data Visualization

Project Overview

This research, conducted as part of the Illinois Blackwell Summer Scholars Program, explores the causal relationships between global climate drivers and local drought conditions. The central goal was to move beyond simple correlation and identify whether specific climate phenomena directly cause changes in drought patterns in Urbana, Illinois. Causal inference is critical in this context, as it helps answer the "why" behind observed events, distinguishing true cause-and-effect links from mere statistical associations.

Data & Variables

The analysis used monthly time-series data from 1950 to 2021. The key variables included:

  • SPEI (Drought Index): The primary variable of interest, measuring drought conditions in Urbana, Illinois.
  • NAO (North Atlantic Oscillation): Measures atmospheric pressure differences over the North Atlantic.
  • ONI (Oceanic Niño Index): Evaluates the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) in the Pacific.
  • PDO (Pacific Decadal Oscillation): Tracks long-term climate variability in the Pacific Ocean.
  • AMO (Atlantic Multi-decadal Oscillation): A cycle impacting sea surface temperatures in the North Atlantic.

Methodology

A three-step methodology was employed to analyze the complex time-series data:

  • 1. Data Smoothing: A moving average filter was applied to the raw data to reduce noise and reveal underlying trends that were otherwise difficult to identify.
  • 2. Correlation Analysis: Cross-Correlation (CCF) was used to measure the linear relationship and time lags between the climate drivers and the drought index. We focused on negative lags to see how a driver might influence drought after a delay.
  • 3. Causal Inference: Convergent Cross Mapping (CCM), a method from Empirical Dynamic Models, was used to test for causality. CCM determines if one variable's history can reliably estimate another's state, which indicates a causal link if predictability increases with more data.

Results & Analysis

Cross-Correlation Findings

CCF analysis identified statistically significant correlations between the drivers and the SPEI drought index at specific time lags. Although the correlation coefficients appear low, their statistical significance confirmed a relationship worth investigating for causality.

Driver Lag (Months) Correlation Coefficient (CC)
NAO -22 0.237
ONI -25 -0.091
PDO -26 -0.096
AMO -8 -0.116

Causal Inference with CCM

The CCM analysis provided much clearer insights into the direction of these relationships.

NAO → SPEI: The results show a clear, one-way causal influence from NAO to SPEI. The reverse relationship (SPEI to NAO) showed no prediction skill.

AMO → SPEI: This was the most well-defined graph, demonstrating a unidirectional causal relationship from AMO to SPEI, though the link remains weak.

PDO → SPEI: A unidirectional relationship from PDO to SPEI was also detected, but the connection is weak.

ONI & SPEI: The CCM plot for ONI did not show convergence, meaning a causal trend could not be determined from this analysis.

Conclusion & Impact

This research concludes that the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), Atlantic Multi-decadal Oscillation (AMO), and Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) have a causal influence on drought conditions (SPEI) in Urbana, Illinois. It highlights that while standard cross-correlation can identify significant relationships, advanced methods like CCM are necessary to determine the direction of causality.

These findings have significant practical applications, particularly for developing predictive machine learning models to forecast drought. Such a tool could be invaluable for agriculture, allowing farmers to prepare for and mitigate the effects of drought.
