Statistical Survey Analysis (STAT 310)

R Survey Data Data Cleaning Inferential Analysis

Project Overview: Assessing Student Office Experiences

This project involved a **comprehensive statistical analysis** of a custom-designed survey targeting **Truman State University Sophomores and Juniors**. The objective was to quantify student satisfaction and experience with various campus offices, providing actionable data for service improvement. This work reinforced the critical importance of meticulous data cleaning, robust statistical testing, and clear visualization in translating raw survey responses into meaningful institutional feedback.

A key defining element of this project was the **direct collaboration with a Truman staff member**, whose role is dedicated to enhancing the overall student experience. We met with this client to define the specific project goals, ensuring the final analysis would directly address their needs for improving campus services and promoting a better quality of life for the student body. Working with survey data presents unique challenges, including missing values and varied data types, which were central to this project.

Client Goal & Project Rationale

The client's primary goal was to gain **detailed, actionable feedback** on student experiences with essential school offices. Given that the target demographic (Sophomores and Juniors) would remain on campus for at least one to two more years, the project's direct impact was immediate: to facilitate changes that would visibly improve service quality and **enhance student satisfaction** at Truman State University.

To achieve this, students were asked to identify up to five of the most frequently visited offices and rate their experiences across key performance indicators (KPIs): **Accessibility, Response Speed, Approachability, Effectiveness, and Overall Satisfaction.**

Methodology and Execution

1. Survey Development and Refinement

  • Stakeholder Engagement: Met with the client to define objectives and scope.
  • Survey Design: Developed a survey instrument informed by research into effective questioning techniques and consultation with faculty (professor meetings).
  • Quality Assurance: Conducted a pilot test and maintained constant team communication to refine question clarity and structure before deployment.

2. Data Preparation and Coding Protocol

  • Data Import & Cleaning: Data was imported into **R**, where extensive cleaning procedures were applied. This included systematic handling of missing values (NAs), correction of data types, and strategic recoding of variables.
  • Coding Protocol: Utilized a **1-to-5 Likert scale** for service ratings and included demographic variables (Academic Year, School of Major) and usage frequency (binary 'Yes/No') coded in **SPSS** for subsequent analysis.

3. Analysis and Interpretation

  • Descriptive Statistics: Calculated summary statistics (mean, median, standard deviation) and frequency distributions to establish a foundational understanding.
  • Data Visualization: Utilized ggplot2 in R to create **histograms, bar charts, and interval plots** to visually represent response distributions and bivariate relationships.
  • Inferential Analysis: Employed **t-tests and ANOVA** to measure statistical significance ($p$-values) and formally test hypotheses regarding student satisfaction.

Key Findings and Impact

  • Visiting Frequency Differentiation: A statistically significant difference was found among the visiting frequencies of the top five offices, confirming that the most-frequented services (e.g., Library, Dining, Financial Aid) were indeed used at measurably different rates than other campus offices.
  • Areas for Improvement: A significant difference was detected among the **overall satisfaction ratings** for the top five offices. This indicated a more dire need for improvement in certain high-traffic areas, specifically highlighting the **Student Union, Rec Center, and Dining Services.**
  • Communication Preference: Students overwhelmingly ranked **email** as the most preferred method of communication with campus offices, followed by in-person discussion, site visits, and phone calls. This finding offers immediate, actionable guidance for improving outreach efficiency.

This project was invaluable for providing hands-on experience in the entire lifecycle of survey data analysis, directly applying statistical models to deliver meaningful, client-focused insights.
